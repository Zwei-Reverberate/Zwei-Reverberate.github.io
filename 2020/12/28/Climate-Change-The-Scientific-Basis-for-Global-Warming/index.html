CH1 温室效应

1.Greenhouse effect

  • 地气系统的能量平衡
    由于微观带电粒子的运动会与电磁场发生相互作用，任何物体都在不断地向外发射电磁波辐射，同时也会吸收电磁波
    地球吸收来自太阳的电磁波辐射，同时也向太空发射红外热辐射
    当地气系统处于平衡状态时，地球吸收的太阳辐射和释放的热辐射相等。当能量平衡被打破时，地球系统的温度将发生变化

  • 温室效应

    太阳短波辐射可以透过大气射入地面，而地面向外发射的长波辐射却被大气部分阻挡，从而使得地表变暖，这个过程被称为温室效应

2.Greenhouse gases and global warming

  • 温室气体
    大气中能吸收地面反射的长波辐射，并重新发射辐射的气体(二氧化碳，甲烷，水蒸气)

  • 温室气体与地表温度
    在其他条件不变的情况下，温室气体浓度越高，温室效应越强，地表温度越高

    温室气体增加是全球变暖的主因
    人类活动

3.Heterogeneity of the greenhouse effect

  • 二氧化碳的水平空间分布

    在近地面，污染严重区域的二氧化碳含量可能高于其他区域，但自由大气中的二氧化碳含量是近似于均一分布的。
    因此，在全球变化分析当中，我们可以近似认为各地区二氧化碳浓度的增加量是相同的。
    然而，尽管二氧化碳的变化是水平均匀的，其温室效应却有着巨大的地域差异。

  • 温室效应的非均一性

    地表温度的水平分布不均一：极地温度低，赤道温度高；热辐射是温度的函数，因此温室效应的幅度与温度相关
    云和水汽的分布不均一：云、水汽对热辐射都有遮蔽作用，因此会影响温室效应的幅度；–赤道地区高云多，极地地区中低云更多

  • 全球温度变化分布的不均一性
    由于辐射强迫和辐射反馈的水平不均一性，全球增温幅度并不相同；而大气环流、降水量的分布则更不均一

  • 蒸发-降水变化的不均匀性

  • 温度、降水变化分布不均一性的影响

    对生态环境的影响：动物的习性、迁移路径将会发生变化。现有生态平衡被破坏。可能会引发战争、蝗灾、饥荒和瘟疫

    气候变暖对不同国家的影响不一样：受气候变化影响较小、而减排任务较大的国家没有强烈减排愿望。受气候变化影响较大、而减排任务较小的国家会积极推动减排。责任与收益的不对等可能会增加全球共同面对气候问题的难度

CH2 气候反馈

1.温室气体与全球变暖的幅度

  • 气候变化幅度对CO2的敏感度

    在没有外界影响的情况下，经过长时间演化以后，地球系统的各个变量会趋于稳定，并在特定值附近振荡。这个状态便被称为气候平衡态
    平衡态气候敏感度：以工业革命前的气候平衡态为基准,当CO2的浓度加倍以后，气候系统经过长期的调整再次达到平衡态时，全球平均地表温度的变化

  • 热辐射反馈

    根据Stefan-Boltzmann定律，物体发射的热辐射与温度的4次方正相关

    如果地球系统的温度均匀增加1度而其他要素不变，地球发射的热辐射增加 3.2W/m^2^

    因此，当温度增加1.1度后，地球辐射会再次达到平衡

    只有热辐射反馈的情况下，平衡态气候敏感度为1.1度

    如果平衡态气候敏感度只有1.1度，全球变暖将不是一个太大的问题

    多方面证据表明，气候敏感度的范围极大概率介于1.5-4.5度之间

2.气候反馈过程

  • 辐射反馈

    当地球能量收支不平衡时，海表温度会慢慢变化

    大气温度、地表温度、云、冰雪覆盖面积、水汽含量等重要变量发生相应的变化

    这些变量的辐射效应改变地球的能量收支，对地球气候系统产生反馈作用

  • 水汽反馈
    大气温度增加$\rightarrow$饱和水汽压增加$\rightarrow$水汽增加$\rightarrow$水汽的温室效应增加$\rightarrow$增强全球变暖(正反馈)

  • 地表返照率反馈

地表温度增加$\rightarrow$冰雪融化$\rightarrow$返照率降低$\rightarrow$吸收更多太阳光(正反馈)

  • 云反馈
    阻挡入射的太阳辐射，冷却作用；减少地球向外热辐射，温室效应

    高云变高：更强的温室效应
    随着温度的增加，气流能够上升到更高的高度;热带深对流云的云顶高度增加;云的温室效应增加，正反馈

    Lindzen的虹膜效应假说
    全球变暖背景下，热带高云会减少；热带高云的温室效应降低；
    Linzen的假说: 这个效应将会对气候变化产生强烈的负反馈，就像虹膜在强光下会收缩瞳孔，保护眼睛一样
    事实：热带高云以后，温室效应降低了，但其反射的太阳光会减少，实际上云辐射效应的变化并不大

    云的冰水相位变化：
    温度升高，冰云变少，水云变多；水云粒子更小，反射太阳光的能力更强；因此，全球增温造成的相位变化会让云反射更多的太阳光，产生负反馈

    低云云量变化
    低云反射大量的太阳光，其温室效应却可以忽略，产生强烈的冷却作用；
    海温增加$\rightarrow$低云云量变少$\rightarrow$低云的冷却作用减少 ,正反馈

  • 温度直减率反馈
    全球变暖的背景下，对流层上层的大气增温大于地表增温；向外发射更多热辐射；对于地球增温产生负反馈作用

  • 碳循环反馈
    冻土融化释放CO2和甲烷；热带雨林的退化；森林大火；特定地区的土壤沙漠化

3.气候反馈与气候敏感度的不确定性

  • 平衡态气候敏感度的不确定性
    IPCC给出的范围: 1.5-4.5K不同文章中的中间值不同，气候模式的结果差异巨大

    观测的不确定性：数据的不确定性很大。

    模式模拟的不确定性：气候反馈的不确定性，特别是云反馈的不确定性

  • 气候反馈与全球变暖
    在水汽反馈、温度直减率反馈、云反馈、冰雪返照率反馈的共同作用下，平衡态气候敏感度的值由1.1度上升到1.5-4.5K，增幅 40%-300%
    而碳循环反馈释放出的额外温室气体，则会使全球变暖的幅度进一步增加。这样，人类活动引发的气候变化的幅度有可能大于气候模式的结果
    云反馈是决定气候敏感度的最大不确定因素

4.气候敏感度与温室气体减排目标
  • 巴黎气候协定
    把全球平均气温升幅控制在工业化前水平以上低于2°C之内；努力将气温升幅限制在工业化前水平以上1.5°C之内
  • 如果气候敏感度较低
    如果气候反馈的总强度较低，平衡态气候敏感度在1.5度附近，我们将排放量降到极低值，那么2度的目标是可能实现的
    如果气候反馈的总强度较高，平衡态气候敏感度接近4K，那么我们必须最终减少现有空气中CO2含量才能够达到巴黎气候协定的目标
  • 减排目标的制定
    在欧洲科学家制定减排目标时，常常使用较低气候敏感度（~2K)。但是最新研究表明，气候敏感度的值更可能较高(>3K),这就意味着这些减排目标可能无法完成任务
    如果使用较高的气候敏感度，升温目标又几乎无法实现，所以巴黎气候协定很难让各国达成减排协议
5.气候反馈与气候突变
  • 气候反馈的非线性
    在较小范围内，我们可以近似认为气候反馈是线性的；在更大的变化范围内，气候反馈并不是线性的；这使得我们无法排除气候突变的风险
  • 外推的巨大不确定性
    在当前气候态下，经观测约束的气候反馈有着较大不确定性
    在其他的气候态下，气候反馈的不确定性则更大，因为我们只能通过线性外推来进行估值
    对于温度远超过或远低于当前温度的气候态，其气候反馈甚至有可能是正值
  • 潜在的气候突变：冰雪地球
    当地球温度降低，冰雪覆盖面积增加的时候，冰雪反照率反馈会增加。当反照率反馈足够大时，气候反馈可能是正值，这会导致地球突然全部冻结
    历史上可能存在冰雪地球时期，但一旦地球冻住很难融化，所以这个说法还存在争议
  • 潜在的未来气候突变
    如果温度上升到一定的值，导致冰雪反馈、水汽反馈、二氧化碳反馈等正反馈的值非常大，气候反馈的值是正的
    当温度增加时，有更多的能量进入地球；温度进一步增加，更多的能量进入地球，温度不停增加下去；气候突变；到达另一个温度远高于当前的平衡态
  • 未来气候突变的风险
    大部分数值模式表明，未来不会发生气候突变；由于模式并不代表真实大气，所以无法排除在未来发生小幅度气候突变的可能性,虽然这个可能性不大；最极端的情况下，如果水蒸汽全部蒸发，地球大气总质量和温室效应将远高于现在，地球可能会变成下一个金星。好在这个最极端的可能性小到可以忽略不计

CH3 雾霾的故事：空气污染

1.Introduction to air pollution

  • Types and Sources of Air Pollution

    • Air Pollution
    – Chemicals added to the atmosphere by natural events or human activities in high enough concentrations to be harmful
    • Two categories
    – Primary Air Pollutant
    • Harmful substance that is emitted directly into the atmosphere
    – Secondary Air Pollutant
    • Harmful substance formed in the atmosphere when a primary air pollutant reacts with substances normally found in the atmosphere or with other air pollutants

  • Particulate Material (PM2.5)

    • Thousands of different solid or liquid particles suspended in air
    – Includes: soil particles, soot, lead, asbestos,sea salt, and sulfuric acid droplets
    • Dangerous for 2 reasons
    – May contain materials with toxic orcarcinogenic effects
    – Extremely small particles can become lodged in lungs

  • Nitrogen and Sulfur Oxides

    • Nitrogen Oxides
    – Gases produced by the chemical interactions between atmospheric nitrogen and oxygen at high temperature
    – Problems
    • Greenhouse gases
    • Cause difficulty breathing

    • Sulfur Oxides
    – Gases produced by the chemical interactionsbetween sulfur and oxygen
    – Causes acid precipitation

  • Carbon Oxides and Hydrocarbons

    • Carbon Oxides
    – Gases carbon monoxide (CO) and carbon
    dioxide (CO 2 )
    – Greenhouse gases

    • Hydrocarbons
    – Diverse group of organic compounds that contain only hydrogen and carbon (ex: CH 4 -methane)
    – Some are related to photochemical smog and greenhouse gases

  • Ozone

    • Tropospheric Ozone
    – Man- made pollutant in the lower atmosphere
    – Secondary air pollutant
    – Component of photochemical smog

    • Stratospheric Ozone
    – Essential component that screens out UV radiation in the upper atmosphere
    – Man- made pollutants (ex: CFCs) can destroy it

2.Effects of air pollution

• Low level exposure
– Irritates eyes
– Causes inflammation of respiratory tract
• Can develop into chronic respiratory diseases

  • Health Effects of Air Pollution

    • Sulfur Dioxide and Particulate material
    – Irritate respiratory tract and impair ability of lungs to exchange gases
    • Nitrogen Dioxides
    – Causes airway restriction
    • Carbon monoxide
    – Binds with iron in blood hemoglobin
    – Causes headache, fatigue, drowsiness, death
    • Ozone
    – Causes burning eyes, coughing, and chest discomfort

  • Children and Air Pollution

    • Greater health threat to children than adults
    – Air pollution can restrict lung development
    – Children breath more often than adults
    • Children who live in high ozone areas are more likely to develop asthma

3.Causes of air pollution

  • Acid Deposition

    Sulfur dioxide and nitrogen dioxide emissions react with water vapor in the atmosphere and form acids that return to the surface as either dry or wet deposition

    Effects of Acid Deposition
    • Declining Aquatic Animal Populations
    • Thin-shelled eggs prevent bird reproduction
    – Because calcium is unavailable in acidic soil
    • Forest decline
    – Ex: Black forest in Germany (50% is destroyed)

  • Natural sources pollute: dust storms

    • Terrestrial Sources.
    – Unsustainable farming and grazing, erosion and desertification
    • Volcanoes
    • Fires
    – Clearing forests for agriculture
    – Burning fossil fuels produces industrial smog
    – Traffic emissions

  • Factors affecting air pollution

    The role of the wind：
    • dilution
    • turbulence
    • mixing

    The Role of Stability and Inversions
    • temperature lapse rates
    • inversions

    The Role of Topography

    • Stagnation (unable to disperse pollutants)

CH4 地球系统模式

Earth System Model

What is Model? A thing used as an example to follow or imitate

  • What is the Earth System Model

    • Earth system models (ESM) seek to simulate all relevantaspects of the Earth system.
    • ESMs are composed by a set of equations describing atmospheric and oceanic circulation and thermodynamics, but also the biological and chemical processes that feedback on to the physics of climate, all solved for a number of locations in space that form a three-dimensional grid over the surface of the Earth and underneath the surface of the oceans
    • Merging all climate-relevant aspects together in a single model is computationally very demanding and time consuming

  • Why do we need to model the Earth System

    • A tool to quantitatively understand how the Earth climate evolve in the past, present and in the future
    • To project future climate changes and their impact
    • To provide scientific supports for future decision making

  • Atmosphere

    大气组分；太阳与地球辐射；地球热量不均匀；三圈环流；海陆热力环流（季风）；天气尺度斜压波；中小尺度对流

CH5 Ocean and Climate Change

  • What is the Ocean’s Role in Climate?

    • the ocean covers 70% of the earth’s surface
    • the ocean stores a thousand times more heat than the atmosphere
    • seawater stores four times more heat per unit mass than air
    • it transports 25 to 50% of the energy received from the sun
    • it circulates slowly, on time scales of decades to centuries
    • it is the major source of heat to some regions like the North Atlantic
    • it sets long-term patterns of atmospheric circulation affecting climate systems like ENSO, NAO

  • Two types of Ocean Circulation

    Surface Circulation – Wind-driven
    Deep Circulation – Density-driven

    Density of water is influenced by Temperature and Salinity, so density- driven circulation is often called the “Thermohaline” Circulation

  • Wind-Driven Ocean Circulation

    Steady winds produce waves and set the surface water in motion
    Moving water is deflected to the right (N.Hemisphere) or left (S.Hemisphere)
    This starts the main “gyre” motion of the surface ocean

So, do the gyres just follow the winds?

Not exactly! But the winds get the motion in the ocean started
The oceans respond by flowing and turning
Water piles up in the center of gyres – several meters high

  • Ekman spiral

    • Surface currents move at angle to wind
    • Ekman spiral describes speed and direction of seawater flow at different depths
    • Each successive layer moves increasingly to right (N hemisphere)

  • Ekman transport

    • Average movement of seawater under influence of wind
    • 90 o to right of wind in Northern hemisphere
    • 90 o to left of wind in Southern hemisphere

  • Thermohaline circulation

    • Below the pycnocline
    • 90% of all ocean water
    • Slow velocity
    • Movement caused by differences in density (temperature and salinity)
    • Cooler seawater denser
    • Saltier seawater denser
    • Originates in high latitude surface ocean
    • Once surface water sinks (high density) it changes little
    • Deep-water masses identified on T-S diagram

  • Deep ocean currents

    •Cold, oxygen-rich surface water to deep ocean
    •Dissolved O 2 important for life and mineral processes
    •Changes in thermohaline circulation can cause global climate change
    • Example, warmer surface waters less dense, not sink, less oxygen deep ocean

  • How the oceans influence climate

    • The oceans influence climate over both long and short time periods
    • The oceans and atmosphere are tightly linked and together form the most dynamic component of the climate system.
    • The oceans play a critical role in storing heat
    • The oceans waters are constantly being moved about by powerful currents.
    • These currents influence the climate by transporting heat
    • Currents involved in “deep-water formation” are particularly important for climate
    • An apparently small change in just one aspect of the oceans behavior can produce major climate variations over large areas of earth.

  • Ocean currents and climate

    •Warm ocean currents warm air at coast
    • Warm, humid air
    • Humid climate on adjoining landmass
    •Cool ocean currents cool air at coast
    • Cool, dry air
    • Dry climate on adjoining landmass

  • Sudden Climate Shifts

    • The ocean conveyor presently heats the North Atlantic.
    • Ice cores reveal past rapid temperature shifts.
    • Must reduce scientific uncertainties of climate “surprises”, e.g. ocean conveyor change or shutdown

  • Atmospheric and oceanic disturbances in Pacific Ocean

    Normal conditions
    • Air pressure across equatorial Pacific is higher in
    eastern Pacific
    • Strong southeast trade winds
    • Pacific warm pool on western side
    • Thermocline deeper on western side
    • Upwelling off the coast of Peru

    El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO)
    • Warm (El Niño) and cold phases (La Niña)
    • High pressure in eastern Pacific weakens
    • Weaker trade winds
    • Warm pool migrates eastward
    • Thermocline deeper in eastern Pacific
    • Downwelling
    • Lower biological productivity
    • Corals particularly sensitive to warmer seawater

    El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO): cool phase (La Niña)
    • Increased pressure difference across equatorial Pacific
    • Stronger trade winds
    • Stronger upwelling in eastern Pacific
    • Shallower thermocline
    • Cooler than normal seawater
    • Higher biological productivity

  • How will the Oceans Change as the Earth Warms?

    • the oceans will warm, sea level will rise
    • its salinity balance will be altered
    • its ability to absorb CO 2 will change

  • What are the Implications?

    The oceans will continue to absorb heat as the climate system warms
    Warming will cause sea water to expand
    The effect of ocean heat absorption will be to delay the warming of the atmosphere
    The rate and magnitude of global warming will be affected by how the ocean circulates in the future – when and where will it release the stored heat?

  • What about Salinity?

    • Oceans contain 97% of Earth’s water
    • Oceans experience 86% of evaporation
    • Oceans receive 78% of planetary precipitation
    • A 1% increase in Atlantic precipitation equals the annual Mississippi runoff
    • A key element of the global water cycle

  • The Water Cycle Will Accelerate With Global Warming

    A warmer atmosphere will carry more water vapor, because of the exponential increase of vapor pressure with temperature.
    An enhanced water cycle will change the distribution of salinity in the upper ocean

CH9 Umbrella effect: the climate effects of aerosols

  • 1816 ： the year without summer

    Global temperature cooled by several degrees
    China, Europe and North America had well-documented below normal temperatures
    • Flood in Europe and China
    • Low temperatures and heavy rains resulted in failed harvests
    • The worst famine of the 19 th mainland Europe
    • Famines around the world (US,China)

  • Aerosol particles

    • An aerosol is defined as a suspension system of solid or liquid particles in a gas. An aerosol includes both the particles and the suspending gas, which is usually air
    • Aerosols are typically of sizes in the range 0.001 μm–10 μm. Aerosols are generated from a wide range of natural and anthropogenic sources (biomass burning, fossil fuel, dust storm, sea spray, volcanic eruption, biogenic production, etc.)

  • Greenhouse effects and umbrella effects
    Greenhouse effects: longwave, greenhouse gases
    Umbrella effects: shortwave, aerosol effects

  • Solar radiation: anything that affects planetary albedo affects incoming radiation

    Planetary albedo: It is defined as the ratio of reflected radiation from the surface to incident radiation upon it. The average overall albedo of Earth, its planetary albedo, is 30 to 35%, because of the covering by clouds, but varies widely locally across the surface, depending on the geological and environmental features
